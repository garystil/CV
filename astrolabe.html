Where is Here?

Astrolabes for future use on Mercury,

Venus, Mars, Jupiter ... and even Pluto

 

Gary James Stilwell

Inspired by Islamic scholar and ancient astronomer Abu Ishaq Ibrahim al-Zarqali, I sketched construction lines to create astrolabes for use on each of the other planets - Mercury, Venus, Mars, Jupiter, Saturn, Uranus and Neptune (as well as Earth and Pluto), supplementing his trigonometric calculations with contemporary NASA data. Variation between the geometric images arises from planetary differences in obliquity to orbit, orbital period and rotational period.

The 'Tablet of al-Zarqali,' a perfected universal astrolabe that could be used at any latitude, sparked my curiosity as to whether the projection effect - the pattern of stars we view from Earth - holds true on the other planets. And, if so, could an astrolabe provide navigational and temporal orientation there as well. The answers - 'Yes' and 'Yes with modifications' - evidence the instrument’s ability to cross between spaces and guide future explorations of our neighbors in space.

An astrolabe is an intricate inclinometer employed by ancient astronomers and navigators to measure the altitude above the horizon of a celestial body. Utilized in Classical Antiquity, the Islamic Golden Age, the European Middle Ages, and the Age of Discovery, it served as an 'analog calculator' to identify stars and planets, determine local latitude given local time (and vice-versa), triangluate, and survey.

The first universal astrolabe was invented by Abu Ishaq Ibrahim al-Zarqali (b. 1029). Unlike its predecessors, his 'Tablet of al-Zarqali' projected both the equatorial and ecliptic coordinate systems on to a vertical plane that cut the celestial sphere at the solstices, permitting its use at any latitude on Earth. It became famous in Europe under its Latin name 'saphea Azarchelis' and, by the late 15th century, had become a part of the astronomical curriculum at European universities.

Al-Zarqali, who originally trained as a metalsmith and earned the nickname Arzachel - 'the engraver of metals' - is also credited with improving the trigonometric tables, calculating the obliquity of the ecliptic, and mapping a stereographic projection of a sphere on a plane.

He stated the orbits of the planets are elliptical, not circular, five centuries before Kepler. In his 'De Revolutionibus Orbium Coelestium,' Copernicus quoted the works of al-Zarqali. He is also referred to in the writings of Chaucer, who believed it was important for his son to understand how to use an astrolabe.

The crater Arzachel on the Moon is named after him.

The multi-planet astrolabe series was launched in the MIT seminar EC.090 'Recreate Experiments from History' led by Elizabeth Cavicchi. Background on astrolabe construction was primarily drawn from James Morrison's book 'The Astrolabe' and Alistair Kwan's unpublished 'Manual for Astrolabe Construction and Use.'

Astronomical data was sourced from NASA's online 'Planetary Fact Sheet.' Latitude was standardized at 42.3736° N (MIT!) for all planets.

The digital sketches were drawn using p5.js, an online JavaScript library developed by Lauren McCarthy and Cassie Tarakajian based on the core principle of Processing as conceived by Media Lab alumni Ben Fry and Casey Reas - to make coding accessible for artists, designers, and educators.

In tribute to Al-Zarqali's metalsmithing skills, the designs were lasered on titanium, copper, aluminum and glass sheets using an Epilog Fusion M2 laser in MIT's makerspace Metropolis.

The astronomical sketches were first presented at the 2018 Radcliffe Institute science symposium 'The Undiscovered' at Harvard University. In December 2018, they were exhibited at MIT's Wiesner Art Gallery as large-scale graphic prints under the title 'Where is Here?' In January 2020, the designs were laser-etched on titanium, copper, aluminum and glass sheets and displayed at MIT’s ProjXpo.

The Scientific Instrument Commission is now considering 'Where is Here? Astrolabes for future use on ...' for presentation at the 26th International Congress of History of Science to be held in Prague in July 2021.
